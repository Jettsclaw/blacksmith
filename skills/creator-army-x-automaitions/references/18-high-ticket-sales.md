# High-Ticket Sales Playbook

The boiler-room and consultative-sales playbook for closing high-ticket offers (agency services, coaching, consulting, B2B SaaS, enterprise) over the phone or video call.

## When to load this file

**Load on demand — NOT on every brief.** Use this file only when the deliverable involves selling a high-ticket offer to a human, by a human, on a call. Specifically:

- Selling automaitions services to other DTC brands ($5K-$50K+ engagements)
- Selling Creator Army / AI Ad Lab managed services (build + run for clients)
- Coaching, consulting, or strategy retainers
- B2B SaaS enterprise sales motion
- Productised consultancy launches with application funnels
- Any offer >$2K where the close happens on a sales call, not a checkout page

**Do NOT load when:**
- Building DTC ad batches (the ad and the checkout do the selling)
- Constructing self-serve funnels under $1K (the funnel closes; no human in the loop)
- Producing organic content (no sales conversation involved)

This file lives in the skill but stays out of the way until human-closed selling enters the picture.

## The chain — where this fits

Sales calls don't happen in isolation. They sit at the end of an indoctrination chain:

```
COLD TRAFFIC
   ↓
APPLICATION / OPT-IN PAGE          ← "Velvet Rope" — applies, doesn't buy
   ↓
INDOCTRINATION SEQUENCE             ← Pattern F from 14-offer-architecture.md
   (3-5 video emails over 2-3 days
    that build authority and handle
    objections BEFORE the call)
   ↓
SALES CALL                          ← THIS FILE
   ↓
CLOSE / ASCEND
```

The sales call works because the prospect has already been pre-sold by the indoctrination sequence. If you're getting on calls with prospects who haven't gone through indoctrination, fix that first — call quality (and close rate) doubles when the prospect arrives warm.

## The Boiler Room call structure — 4 phases

Cardone's commission-driven sales teams follow a rigid 4-phase structure that closes up to 80% of warm leads. The same structure applies to consultative sales — the *delivery* is different, the *structure* is the same.

```
PHASE 1 — DIAGNOSIS                 (uncover pain + qualify)        [10-15 min]
PHASE 2 — AMPLIFY                   (cost of inaction)              [5-10 min]
PHASE 3 — PRESENT                   (the high-ticket offer)         [10-15 min]
PHASE 4 — CLOSE                     (handle objections + commit)    [10-20 min]
                                                            TOTAL:  [45-60 min]
```

### Phase 1 — Diagnosis (the most important phase)

The diagnostic phase is where 90% of the close happens. By the end of it, the prospect should be telling you their problem in their own words — and they should believe you understand it better than they do.

**Opening (2 min):**
- Confirm the appointment was for X minutes
- "I've reviewed your application and watched a few of your videos / looked at your site. Before I dive in, I want to make sure I understand the full picture. Mind if I ask you a few questions first?"
- Set the frame: *"My job on this call is to figure out if there's a fit — both ways. If there's not, I'll tell you straight up. If there is, I'll show you exactly what working together looks like and how much it costs."*

**Diagnostic questions (10-12 min):**
The questions structure depends on what you're selling, but the categories are constant:

| Question category | Examples | What you're listening for |
|---|---|---|
| **Current state** | "Walk me through what you're doing right now for [the thing your service does]." | Specifics — vague answers = unqualified or under-aware prospect |
| **Pain / cost** | "What's the biggest frustration with how it's going?" / "What's that costing you?" | Quantify the pain — in dollars, hours, opportunity cost |
| **What they've tried** | "What have you already tried? What worked, what didn't?" | Their belief state about your category — have they been burned? |
| **Desired state** | "If we fast-forwarded 6 months and this was working perfectly, what would that look like?" | Vision — pull them into the after-state mentally |
| **Why now** | "What changed recently that made you book this call?" | The trigger — the actual event that opened the buying window |
| **Decision process** | "Who else is involved in a decision like this? What's your decision-making process?" | Identify hidden stakeholders + budget authority |
| **Budget framing** | "Without committing to anything, what kind of investment have you thought about for solving this?" | Set the price anchor before YOU set it |

**Tone:** consultative, not interrogative. You're a doctor diagnosing, not a salesperson qualifying.

**Critical rule:** the prospect should be talking 70% of this phase. If you're talking more than 30%, you're not diagnosing — you're presenting too early.

### Phase 2 — Amplify (cost of inaction)

Now you've heard their pain. Before you present a solution, you make the cost of NOT solving it explicit.

**Pattern:**
1. Reflect the pain back in their words: *"So if I'm hearing you right — you're spending $X/month on Y, getting Z results, and what you actually want is W. Is that fair?"*
2. Quantify the cost: *"What happens if this doesn't change in the next 6 months? 12 months?"*
3. Push to specifics: *"You mentioned [specific metric] is at [X]. If we 2x'd that, what would that mean for [the bigger picture]?"*
4. Plant the urgency: *"What's the cost of waiting another quarter to fix this?"*

The amplification phase moves the prospect from "this is annoying" to "this is unacceptable." Without that shift, your offer feels expensive. With it, your offer feels cheap.

**Cardone version:** aggressive, leans on fear. *"If you don't fix this, your competitors will eat you alive."*

**Consultative version:** honest, leans on opportunity. *"The window for [X] is open now and closing — every quarter you wait is compounding."*

Pick your register based on the prospect's emotional state. Some prospects need fear; others tune it out.

### Phase 3 — Present (the high-ticket offer)

Now and ONLY now do you present the offer. The presentation should take 10-15 minutes max — any longer and you're over-selling.

**Structure:**

1. **Bridge** (30s): *"Based on everything you've told me, here's what I think makes sense for you..."*
2. **What it is** (2 min): The offer. Specifically. Concrete deliverables, timeline, what's included.
3. **The Hero Mechanism** (2 min): WHY this works when others don't. Cross-reference `14-offer-architecture.md` → Hero Mechanism. *"The reason we get [outcome] when [competitors] don't is [mechanism]."*
4. **Proof** (3 min): Case studies, results, testimonials — specifically from prospects who looked like THEM (same industry, same stage, same pain).
5. **What they get** (3 min): The value stack — every deliverable named, priced, totaled. *"You're getting [X], [Y], [Z], plus [bonus 1], [bonus 2] — total value [$total]."*
6. **The investment** (1 min): Reveal the price. Frame against the value stack and the cost of inaction. *"That's normally [$X]. For new clients this quarter we're offering it at [$Y] — and given what we just talked about, you'd recoup that in [timeframe]."*
7. **The guarantee** (1 min): Power guarantee from `14-offer-architecture.md` Component 6. Specific, measurable, attached to outcome.
8. **The ask** (30s): *"Does that sound like the right fit for what we just discussed?"*

**Don't:**
- Discount in the presentation (discount only as an objection-handler if needed)
- Talk past the price reveal — say the number, then stop talking and let them respond
- Pile on more value after the price (signals weakness — your initial value stack should be enough)

### Phase 4 — Close (handle objections + commit)

The prospect WILL object. That's not a problem — it's the close. Objections aren't rejection; they're requests for more information about specific concerns.

See "The 8 Common Objections" section below for specific rebuttals.

**General close structure:**

1. **Listen to the objection fully** (don't interrupt)
2. **Acknowledge it** ("That's a fair question / I hear that a lot / I'd be thinking the same thing")
3. **Isolate it** ("If we addressed [X], is there anything else that would hold you back?") — this exposes hidden objections
4. **Address it** (specific rebuttal — see below)
5. **Confirm resolution** ("Does that address your concern?")
6. **Re-ask for the commitment** ("So shall we get you started today?")

**The first-call commitment rule:** Cardone's teams aim to close on the first call. Reason: every day between first call and close, close rate drops 10-20%. *"I'd love to think about it"* is the kiss of death — once they're off the call, they're in the world full of other priorities and the urgency dies.

The consultative version: don't pressure for an immediate yes, but DO set a clear next step before ending the call. *"I want you to make this decision properly — but let's get a follow-up call scheduled for [specific day/time]. Can we lock that in now?"*

## The 8 Common Objections (with rebuttal frameworks)

Every objection falls into one of these 8 categories. Pre-rehearse rebuttals so you're not improvising under pressure.

### 1. "I don't have the money / It's too expensive"

**What it usually means:** Either (a) they genuinely can't afford it, or (b) they don't see enough value to justify the price, or (c) they're testing you.

**Rebuttals:**
- **Reframe to value:** *"I hear you. Quick question — if I could show you how to recoup the investment in 60 days, would the price still be the issue, or is there something else?"*
- **Cost of inaction:** *"You mentioned [pain] is costing you $X/month. So the question isn't 'can I afford this?' — it's 'can I afford to keep losing $X/month?'"*
- **Payment plan offer:** *"What if we broke it into 3 monthly payments instead of one? Would that work?"*
- **Isolate:** *"If money wasn't the issue, would you be in?"* (exposes whether it's REALLY money or something else)

### 2. "I need to talk to my spouse / business partner"

**What it usually means:** Either (a) genuine — they actually need to consult, or (b) classic stall.

**Rebuttals:**
- **Pre-empt in Phase 1:** Always ask early *"Who else is involved in a decision like this?"* If they say "just me," reference that later.
- **3-way call:** *"Totally understand. Can we get them on a call together — I can answer their questions directly so it's not lost in translation."*
- **Frame the conversation:** *"What questions do you think they'll have? Let me give you the answers so you're prepared to have that conversation."*
- **Set the next step:** *"When are you talking to them? Let's lock in a follow-up call for the day after."*

### 3. "I need to think about it"

**What it usually means:** Either (a) genuine deliberation, or (b) the prospect couldn't say no directly but the answer is no.

**Rebuttals:**
- **Isolate:** *"That's fair. Can I ask — what specifically do you need to think about? Maybe I can help."*
- **Surface the concern:** *"In my experience, 'I need to think about it' usually means there's one specific thing that doesn't sit right yet. What is it?"*
- **Time-box:** *"Take 24 hours. I'll send a quick summary email with the offer details. Can we get a follow-up call locked in for [specific time] tomorrow?"*

**Do NOT** accept "I'll let you know" — it's the death of the deal. Always set a specific follow-up commitment.

### 4. "I don't have the time to implement this"

**What it usually means:** Genuine concern about effort, OR a polite way of saying "I'm not committed enough."

**Rebuttals:**
- **Done-for-you reframe:** *"That's exactly why this works for people like you — we do [X, Y, Z] for you. Your only job is [minimal commitment]."*
- **Cost of NOT doing it:** *"How much time are you currently spending on [the broken thing]? This will give you that time back."*
- **Compare:** *"The clients getting the best results spend [X] hours/week on this. Is that something you can commit to?"*

### 5. "Let me get back to you / send me more info"

**What it usually means:** Almost always means no, or a polite stall.

**Rebuttals:**
- **Direct:** *"Honestly, in my experience when someone says that, it usually means they're not 100% sure this is the right fit. What's the concern?"*
- **Information audit:** *"I can send anything you need. Specifically — what information would help you make the decision?"* (forces specificity)
- **Set commitment:** *"I'll send the info today. Let's lock in a 15-min follow-up call for [day] to make the call together."*

### 6. "I'm already doing something similar"

**What it usually means:** Either (a) they ARE — in which case qualify whether they're happy, or (b) deflection.

**Rebuttals:**
- **Compare:** *"What's working about your current approach? What isn't?"* (uncovers gaps)
- **Differentiation:** *"How is what you're doing similar to what I described? Because [our mechanism] is specifically designed to solve [the gap they just mentioned]."*
- **Switch story:** *"Most of our best clients came from [competitor / similar approach]. Here's why they switched: [specific reason]."*

### 7. "I'm not sure if I'm ready / I need to prepare first"

**What it usually means:** Fear-based hesitation. The prospect doesn't believe they can execute.

**Rebuttals:**
- **Reframe readiness:** *"Honestly, nobody is ever 100% ready. The clients who get the best results are the ones who jump in and figure it out — we help them figure it out."*
- **Belief building:** *"What would 'being ready' look like? Because everything you've described tells me you're more ready than you think."*
- **Reduce stakes:** *"The guarantee means if you start and it's not working, you get refunded. So there's no 'wrong time' to start — only 'not starting at all' which keeps you stuck where you are."*

### 8. "Your competitor is cheaper"

**What it usually means:** Price shopping. Often a soft test.

**Rebuttals:**
- **Reframe to ROI:** *"Cheaper to buy isn't the same as cheaper to USE. What's the cost difference? Let me show you the ROI difference."*
- **Differentiation:** *"What does [competitor] include? Because we include [X, Y, Z] which they don't — that alone is worth [$amount]."*
- **Quality frame:** *"The reason we're priced where we are is [mechanism / track record]. If you went with [cheaper option] and it didn't work, what would that cost you in time and momentum?"*

## 5 Closing techniques

Pick the close that matches the prospect's state at the end of Phase 4.

### 1. Assumptive close
For prospects who are 90%+ ready but haven't said the word.

*"Great — let me get you set up. What's the best email for the contract?"*

### 2. Alternative close
For prospects who are deciding between options.

*"Do you want to start with the [option A] or the [option B]?"*

Reframes the decision from "yes/no" to "which one."

### 3. Summary close
For prospects who need their concerns reframed before commitment.

*"Let me recap. You said [pain], you've tried [failed solutions], you want [outcome]. We're going to deliver that through [mechanism], with a [guarantee], for [investment]. Does that match what you want?"*

### 4. The "yes ladder"
For prospects who are tentative — build commitment through small yeses.

*"Are you confident the problem is real? [yes] Are you confident we can solve it? [yes] Are you confident the investment is fair? [yes] Then let's get started."*

### 5. Direct close
For decisive prospects who want directness.

*"Are you in?"*

## Application — selling automaitions services to other companies

Your specific use case. The automaitions skill + the AI Ad Lab suite is a $10K-$50K/month managed-services offer for DTC brands. Here's how this file applies:

### Pre-call indoctrination (Pattern F from `14-offer-architecture.md`)

5-video sequence sent over 3-5 days after they apply:
1. **Founder origin** — why you built automaitions / how you got into the AI Ad Lab work
2. **Mechanism** — how the suite works (chain map from `16-skill-orchestration.md`). Frame it as a 25-skill production engine, not "we use ChatGPT."
3. **Proof** — case study from one DTC brand you've run this for. Real numbers (CAC before/after, AOV shift, creative volume).
4. **Objection handler** — the top 2-3 things DTC founders say ("I have an agency already," "I'm already running ads," "We need to test internally first") with your pre-baked response.
5. **The offer** — Godfather Offer paragraph. Specific deliverable, timeline, investment, guarantee.

### Diagnostic questions for DTC founders

Specific to your buyer:

| Question | What you're listening for |
|---|---|
| "What's your monthly Meta spend right now?" | Qualifies fit ($10K+/month minimum) |
| "How many creatives do you ship per month currently?" | Their bottleneck (most ship <10; suite ships 40+) |
| "What's your current process for producing static ads / videos / landing pages?" | Where the pain is (usually: slow, expensive, freelancer-dependent) |
| "Who's writing your ad copy?" | Often the founder themselves — pain point |
| "When was the last time you tested a new offer vs. a new creative?" | If they only test creative, you have the Strike Anywhere case study ready |
| "What's your AOV? Sub LTV?" | Tells you whether subscribe-and-save or one-time-purchase architecture matters |
| "What does your landing page conversion look like vs. your ad CTR?" | If LP conversion is the bottleneck, automaitions is the lead-with offer |

### Pain amplification for DTC founders

The pains to amplify (specifically for DTC founders considering bringing the AI Ad Lab in):
- Creative production cost (freelancer team = $5-15K/month for 10-20 creatives/month; you do 40+ for less)
- Time to ship (3-4 weeks for a UGC ad with traditional production; days with AI UGC)
- Inability to test offers because page rebuilds are slow (automaitions ships landing pages in hours)
- Andromeda penalty for near-duplicate creative (multiplier solves this)
- Founder bottleneck on creative direction (CA encodes the strategy)

### The presentation

Don't position yourself as "an AI tool" — position as "a 25-skill production engine, plus the strategic brain that operates it." The Hero Mechanism is the chain itself (research → strategy → production → distribution → learning), not any single tool.

Pricing examples (your call on actual numbers):
- **Done-for-you batch:** $10-25K per monthly batch (40+ creatives, fresh offers, LP updates)
- **Retainer:** $15-50K/month for ongoing production + strategic reviews
- **Strategic audit + setup:** $5-10K one-time, then optional retainer
- **License the suite + train your team:** $25-100K (productised consultancy)

### Common DTC objections + your rebuttals

- **"We already have an agency."** *"What are they producing per month? What are they charging? Most agencies bill $8-15K/month for 5-10 ads. We produce 40+ for similar money, with the strategic layer they don't have."*
- **"AI ads don't convert like real UGC."** *"Show me one ad you're running that's been performing for 60+ days. I bet I can rebuild it via /rebuild with persona variations and outperform it within 14 days — and if I can't, you pay nothing."*
- **"My team can use ChatGPT."** *"ChatGPT writes copy. We have a 25-skill production engine that does customer research, brand DNA, ad scripting, image generation, video production, landing page builds, and Meta bulk upload — all governed by a strategic brain that ensures everything converts. ChatGPT is one component of one of those skills."*
- **"We need to test it internally first."** *"Fair. Want to do a paid 30-day pilot? You give us one product, we produce 40+ creatives. If we don't beat your current control by 20% on hook rate, we refund the pilot fee."*

## Ethical guardrails — Cardone aggressive vs. consultative honest

The Cardone playbook works but the reputational risk is real (see ethical guardrails table in `14-offer-architecture.md`). Same applies to sales scripts:

| Move | Aggressive (Cardone) | Consultative (recommended) |
|---|---|---|
| Diagnostic questions | Lead them to predetermined answers | Genuinely diagnose; willing to disqualify |
| Pain amplification | Maximise fear regardless of fit | Quantify honestly; let the math speak |
| Price reveal | After 30 minutes of pre-selling | After the value is clearly understood — don't hide |
| Objection handling | Pre-rehearsed pressure rebuttals | Acknowledge → isolate → address → confirm |
| Close on first call | Always — willing to burn the lead | Encouraged but not at the cost of trust |
| Follow-up after rejection | Aggressive nurture sequence trying to reopen | One follow-up, then respect the no |
| Refund handling | Discourage / make hard | Easy, automatic, builds reputation |

**Choose your register.** Both close deals. The consultative version generates 2-5x referral revenue and protects your reputation; the aggressive version closes faster short-term but burns leads and creates negative word-of-mouth. For your situation (selling agency services where word-of-mouth in the DTC community is your CAC engine), consultative wins.

## Pre-call + on-call checklist

Before every sales call:

- [ ] Read the prospect's application thoroughly
- [ ] Check their website, recent ads (`/spy` on their brand), social
- [ ] Identify which Hero Mechanism / case study to lead with based on their stage
- [ ] Pre-write 3-4 diagnostic questions specific to their business
- [ ] Have the Godfather Offer paragraph ready (don't improvise)
- [ ] Have 2-3 case studies queued (matched to their industry / stage)
- [ ] Know the price tier you'll quote and the floor you won't go below
- [ ] Block 60 minutes (calls run long when they go well)
- [ ] Set the follow-up email template to send within 30 min of the call

On the call:

- [ ] Phase 1 — Diagnose (10-15 min, prospect talks 70%)
- [ ] Phase 2 — Amplify (5-10 min, quantify the cost of inaction)
- [ ] Phase 3 — Present (10-15 min, with mechanism + proof + value stack)
- [ ] Phase 4 — Close (10-20 min, handle objections with the 8 rebuttals)
- [ ] Either close OR set a SPECIFIC follow-up call (date, time, agenda)
- [ ] Send recap email within 30 min

## The one-liner

**Diagnose first (prospect talks 70%). Amplify the cost of inaction. Present the mechanism + proof + value stack + guarantee. Pre-rehearse the 8 common objections. Close on the first call when possible; set a specific follow-up when not. Consultative wins long-term in word-of-mouth markets like DTC.**

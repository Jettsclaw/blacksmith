# The Selling Sequence — Deep Guide

The selling sequence maps face-to-face selling instincts to short-form video.
This is how Simon Beard sold at Carrara Markets on the Gold Coast — eye contact,
intrigue, handle objections, demonstrate, close. The medium changed. The
psychology didn't.

## The Sequence Applied to Short-Form Video

### 1. RAPPORT (0-3 seconds)

**Market stall equivalent:** Making eye contact. A nod. A smile. A comment
about their shoes. Something that says "I see you" before any selling happens.

**In video:** The first frame must create recognition. The viewer thinks
"this is for me" or "I relate to this person" or "wait, what?"

**Three rapport triggers (use at least one):**

**FEEL:** Hit an emotion in the first frame.
- Fear: "Your $50 moisturiser is making your acne worse"
- FOMO: "Almost sold out — here's why everyone's buying this"
- Humor: Boyfriend stealing skincare, pet doing something dumb
- Shock: Unexpected visual, bold claim, controversy
- Nostalgia: "Remember when [shared experience]..."

**LOOK:** Stop the thumb with something visual.
- Movement: fast motion, unexpected camera angle, object toss
- ASMR: satisfying sounds, textures, unboxing
- Colour: bright against muted, contrast, neon
- Pattern interrupt: something physically unexpected in the frame

**THINK:** Plant a thought that demands resolution.
- "Does your boyfriend always use your skincare?"
- "I took mushrooms for 30 days and here's what happened"
- "Number one worst fruit for your waistline"
- "I spent $10,000 testing every protein powder. Here's what I found."

### 2. OPEN LOOP (3-10 seconds)

**Market stall equivalent:** "Want to see something cool?" while reaching
for the product but NOT showing it yet. The customer leans in.

**In video:** Create a question the viewer CANNOT close without watching.
The open loop is the gap between "I'm curious" and "now I know." If you
close it too early, they leave. If you never close it, they feel cheated.

**Open loop structures:**

**The Promise:** "I'm going to show you the one thing that changed everything"
— viewer watches to find out what the "one thing" is.

**The Tease:** "This looks like a normal [X] but watch what happens when..."
— viewer watches to see the transformation.

**The Contradiction:** "Everyone says [common belief]. They're wrong. Here's why."
— viewer watches to resolve the cognitive dissonance.

**The Story Entry:** "Last week something happened that I need to tell you about"
— viewer watches because humans are hardwired for stories.

**The Challenge:** "I tried your brand's product for 30 days. Here's my honest review."
— viewer watches for the verdict.

### 3. STACK VALUE + OVERCOME OBJECTIONS (10-40 seconds)

**Market stall equivalent:** The pitch. But not a monologue — a conversation.
You're reading the customer's face, adjusting in real time, stacking reasons
to buy while watching for the flinch that tells you what their objection is.

**In video:** Stack 3-5 building blocks without repeating yourself. Cut
anything that drags. Show don't tell when possible.

**Stacking rules:**
- Each building block should take 5-8 seconds max
- Alternate between emotional and rational blocks
- If you show a problem, immediately follow with the solution
- If you make a claim, immediately follow with proof
- Never say the same thing twice with different words

**Common stacking sequences:**

**For skeptical viewers (tried other products):**
Problem → Failed Solution → Unique Mechanism → Demo → Social Proof

**For unaware viewers (don't know they need this):**
Relatable Scenario → Problem Reveal → Desired Result → Solution → CTA

**For price-sensitive viewers:**
Value Stack → Social Proof → Risk Reversal → Limited Offer → CTA

**For impulse buyers:**
Demo/Visual Proof → Social Proof → Scarcity → CTA

### 4. WOW / AHA MOMENT (30-45 seconds)

**Market stall equivalent:** The demo. The moment you flip the product
over and the customer's eyes widen. "Oh, it does THAT too?"

**In video:** A single moment of belief shift. Not incremental — a jump.

**Wow moment types:**

**The Metaphor:** Makes the complex instantly clear.
- "Skincare for acne is like painting a dead leaf green"
- "You can go fishing, but without the right bait you won't catch anything"
- "Comparing cloud storage to a virtual filing cabinet"
These work because they bypass rational processing and land instantly.

**The Live Demo:** Shows the product working in real time.
- Before/after in one continuous shot (no cuts = trust)
- Side-by-side comparison with competitor
- Unexpected use case ("I didn't know it could do that")

**The Customer Reaction:** Genuine, unscripted response.
- Unboxing with real first-time reaction
- Reading their own blood test results after using the product
- Video call reaction to their own transformation photos

**The Data Bomb:** One number that changes everything.
- "37% lower risk of dying from any cause"
- "42,000 jars ordered in a matter of hours"
- "We went from $0 to $15K a day in 14 days"

### 5. CLEAR NEXT STEP (Final 3-5 seconds)

**Market stall equivalent:** "Cash or card?" Not "would you like to buy
this?" The assumption of the sale.

**In video:** Make the next action obvious and LOW FRICTION.

**CTA approaches:**
- **Direct:** "Link in bio" / "Tap the link" / "Shop now"
- **Urgency:** "This deal ends Friday" / "Only 200 left"
- **Community:** "We heard you — here's a special bundle we put together"
- **Soft:** "Check out the blog for the full breakdown" / "Take the quiz"
- **Engagement:** "Comment DEAL and I'll DM you the link"

**The leaked discount code trick:** Create content around a "leaked" or
community-requested discount. It feels organic, not salesy. Example:
customer comments "you should bundle these two products" → brand responds
"hey, we hear you, we put this together, it's not going to last forever."

**If the CTA feels forced, the body didn't do enough work.** A strong
selling sequence makes the CTA feel like permission, not pressure.

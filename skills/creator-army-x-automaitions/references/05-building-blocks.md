# The 10 Building Blocks of Persuasion

These are the modular components you stack in the body of any ad. Use 3-5
per piece of content. Order them based on your customer type's belief state.

## The Blocks

### 1. PROBLEM
Make them FEEL the problem, not just understand it. Emotional buyers
make decisions first, then justify with logic.

- "You know that feeling when you wake up and your skin looks worse than yesterday?"
- Front-load in the hook when the viewer is problem-aware
- Use specific, sensory language (not "skin problems" but "that dry, flaky patch")

### 2. FAILED SOLUTION
What they tried that didn't work. This adds social proof AND positions
your product as the answer to specific failures.

- "I tried every cream on the shelf. Nothing worked."
- "We hired three agencies. All promised results. None delivered."
- The more specific the failed solution, the more credible your alternative

### 3. DESIRED RESULTS
Paint the PICTURE of the outcome. Use descriptive, aspirational words.
Make them see themselves in the after state.

- "Imagine waking up, looking in the mirror, and actually liking what you see"
- "Clear, glowing skin that you don't have to cover up with makeup"
- Show don't tell when possible — visuals of the outcome beat descriptions

### 4. SOLUTION
Your product or category. Why it's different from what they tried.
This is NOT a feature list — it's the MECHANISM that makes it work.

- "Unlike topical treatments, this works from the inside out"
- "One campaign, broad targeting, let the AI find your customers"
- Lead with the unique mechanism, not the product name

### 5. SOCIAL PROOF
Reviews, testimonials, numbers, community evidence. The scrappier
the better — a screenshot from Trustpilot beats a designed graphic.

- Screenshot of real reviews (not designed — raw screenshots)
- Customer video testimonials (unscripted, genuine reactions)
- Numbers: "47,000 five-star reviews" / "sold 42,000 jars in hours"
- Before/after photos from real customers (with permission)

### 6. DEMO / VISUAL PROOF
Show the product working. Show don't tell is the single most
underused principle in ad creative.

- Live application of skincare product (texture, absorption)
- Side-by-side comparison (your product vs competitor)
- Time-lapse of results (Day 1 vs Day 30)
- Screen recording of software actually working
- If you can SHOW it, never just SAY it

### 7. UNIQUE MECHANISM
The scientific or structural reason YOUR solution works when others
didn't. This is the "why" behind the "what."

- "Contains postbiotics that survive stomach acid — most probiotics don't"
- "Our AI analyzes 10,000 data points per ad — a human media buyer checks 5"
- The mechanism should feel like a discovery, not a sales claim

**Deep methodology:** Mechanism is the single highest-leverage block in this
list. The full 5-step identification process (Schwartz heritage + Sabri Suby's
"Hero Mechanism" framing) lives in `14-offer-architecture.md` → **Hero
Mechanism** section. Read it before building any new creative for a new brand —
the mechanism gets decided once per brand and then drives every piece of
creative until competitive pressure forces a refresh.

### 8. RISK REVERSAL
Remove the risk of trying. The stronger the guarantee, the lower
the barrier to purchase.

- "90-day money back guarantee, no questions asked"
- "Try it for 30 days. If you don't see results, we'll refund every cent"
- "Free returns, free shipping, no subscriptions"
- State the guarantee as CONFIDENCE, not as a safety net

### 9. SCARCITY
Genuine scarcity converts. Fake scarcity erodes trust. Use real
constraints — limited batches, seasonal ingredients, capacity limits.

- "We only have 200 units of this batch"
- "This bundle ends Friday at midnight"
- Community-driven: "You guys asked for this, so we put it together.
  It's not going to last forever."
- Time-limited offers with real expiry dates

### 10. AUTHORITY
Expert endorsement, press coverage, awards, certifications. Anything
that borrows credibility from a trusted third party.

- "As featured in Men's Health"
- "Recommended by Dr. Lisa" (real expert with real credentials)
- Awards, certifications, lab test results
- Partnership with known brand or personality

## Stacking Sequences by Customer Type

### Skeptic (tried before, didn't work)
`Failed Solution → Unique Mechanism → Demo → Social Proof → Risk Reversal`

### Unaware (doesn't know they need this)
`Problem → Desired Results → Solution → Social Proof → CTA`

### Price-sensitive (wants a deal)
`Social Proof → Value Stack → Risk Reversal → Scarcity → CTA`

### Impulse buyer (ready to act)
`Demo → Social Proof → Scarcity → CTA`

### Brand-loyal (already knows you)
`New Product/Feature → Demo → Community → Limited Offer`

## The Persuasion Cross-Check (Aristotle + Mowbray)

After stacking blocks, verify the ad hits ALL five pillars:

1. **Ethos (Credibility):** Is there a reason to trust this source?
2. **Logos (Proof):** Is there data, demonstration, or evidence?
3. **Pathos (Emotion):** Does it make you FEEL something?
4. **Metaphor (Clarity):** Is the concept instantly understandable?
5. **Brevity:** Can you cut 30% without losing impact? If yes, cut it.

If any pillar is missing, the ad has a weak point competitors will exploit.

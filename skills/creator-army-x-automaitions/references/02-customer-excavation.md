# Customer Excavation

Before generating any creative, run this process. Skip it and every ad you
make will be generic. Do it properly and every ad will speak directly to a
real human with a real problem.

## When to use this file vs. `/voc`

**`/voc` is the production skill** that does customer research automatically —
40+ verbatim quotes, 16-section HTML doc with Schwartz awareness tags and
JTBD Force classification. If the brand has a recent `/voc` doc, use it
(see `16-skill-orchestration.md` Appendix A for how to read it strategically).

**This file is for:**
- Brands where `/voc` hasn't been run yet and the user wants a quick manual pass
- Gap-filling specific dimensions a `/voc` doc didn't cover well
- Understanding the strategic LOGIC behind the questions `/voc` asks
- Cases where the user wants to walk through excavation in conversation
  rather than commission a research doc

For any serious brand investment, run `/voc` first and use this file as
the strategic interpretation layer.

## The Core Principle

Creative IS your targeting. You don't target "women 25-44 interested in
skincare." You make an ad that speaks to a woman who's tried three acne
treatments that didn't work, and Meta's AI finds her for you.

But to make that ad, you need to KNOW that woman exists. That's what
customer excavation gives you.

## Step 1: Identify Barriers to Purchase

Barriers are NOT just "price is too high." Barriers are the real, often
unspoken reasons someone sees your product and doesn't buy.

Ask these questions about the PRODUCT CATEGORY, not just your brand:

### Category Barriers (what stops them from buying ANY product like yours)
- "I've tried something like this before and it didn't work"
- "I don't believe the claims"
- "I don't know if it works for my specific situation"
- "I don't understand how to use it"
- "It feels too good to be true"
- "I'm not the type of person who buys this"

### Brand-Specific Barriers (what stops them from buying YOUR product)
- "I've never heard of this brand"
- "It looks too cheap/expensive"
- "I can't find reviews from people like me"
- "The website looks sketchy"
- "I don't know anyone who uses it"

### Timing Barriers (what stops them from buying RIGHT NOW)
- "I'm not ready yet"
- "I need to think about it"
- "I want to wait for a sale"
- "I need to check with someone first"

**Each barrier = at least one ad angle.** An ad that directly addresses
"I've tried this before and it didn't work" is a completely different ad
from one that addresses "I've never heard of this brand."

## Step 2: Identify Motivators

Motivators are the emotional and rational reasons someone WANTS to buy.
Again, think about the category AND your specific brand.

### Emotional Motivators
- What does the person FEEL when they use a product like this?
- What pain are they trying to escape?
- What version of themselves are they trying to become?
- Who do they want to impress or make proud?

### Rational Motivators
- What specific outcome are they buying?
- What metric would they use to judge success?
- What feature matters most to their specific situation?
- What's the ROI or cost-per-use calculation?

### Social Motivators
- Do they want to be seen using this?
- Is there a community or identity attached to this product?
- Would they tell friends about it? Why?

## Step 3: Map Customer Types

You don't have ONE customer. You have 3-5 distinct types who buy for
completely different reasons.

### Template for Each Customer Type:
```
Name: [give them a name — "Frustrated Fiona" or "Skeptical Steve"]
Core belief: [what do they believe RIGHT NOW about this category?]
Core barrier: [the #1 thing stopping them]
Core motivator: [the #1 thing pulling them toward buying]
Proof they need: [what would change their mind?]
Content angle: [what ad would speak directly to THIS person?]
```

### Example (Natural Acne Supplement):

**Type 1: "Tried Everything Tina"**
- Belief: "I've tried every product and nothing works for me"
- Barrier: Category skepticism — she's been burned before
- Motivator: Desperate for something that actually works
- Proof: Before/after from someone with her exact skin type
- Angle: "Failed solution" hook — address what didn't work and why this is different

**Type 2: "Chemical-Free Clara"**
- Belief: "Most skincare is full of chemicals that make it worse"
- Barrier: Ingredient trust — needs to know exactly what's in it
- Motivator: Wants a natural solution that aligns with her values
- Proof: Ingredient transparency, certification, founder story
- Angle: "Inside the formula" hook — show every ingredient and why

**Type 3: "Quick Fix Quinn"**
- Belief: "I just want this gone before Saturday"
- Barrier: Time — doesn't want a 90-day journey
- Motivator: Speed of results
- Proof: Timeline testimonials ("I saw results in 5 days")
- Angle: "Speed" hook — focus on how fast it works

## Step 4: Generate the Creative Matrix

For each customer type, create at least 3 ad angles:

| Customer Type | Angle 1 | Angle 2 | Angle 3 |
|--------------|---------|---------|---------|
| Tried Everything | Failed solution story | Side-by-side comparison | Customer transformation |
| Chemical-Free | Ingredient deep dive | Founder "why I made this" | Expert endorsement |
| Quick Fix | Timeline testimonial | Before/after speed test | "Day 1 vs Day 5" content |

This gives you 9+ unique creative angles from one product. Each one will
find a different audience through Meta's AI. This is creative volume done
RIGHT — not 9 versions of the same ad, but 9 genuinely different approaches.

## When the User Hasn't Done This Work

If someone says "write me an ad" without providing barriers, motivators,
or customer types, DO NOT just write a generic ad.

Instead, ask:
1. "What's the #1 reason someone sees your product and doesn't buy?"
2. "What are the 2-3 different types of people who buy from you?"
3. "What has your customer tried before that didn't work?"
4. "What do your best reviews say? What specific words do customers use?"

These four questions give you enough to generate targeted creative.
